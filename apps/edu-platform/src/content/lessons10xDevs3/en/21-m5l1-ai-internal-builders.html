In the fourth module, we looked inside the project: how to manage legacy, how to extract the domain, how to find a sensible modernization direction, and how not to make the agent refactor everything at once.

In the fifth module, we change the perspective.

We no longer just ask: "how to improve my project?". We ask: "what in the team's work regularly wastes time, attention, and energy?". Because when code starts being created faster thanks to AI, it quickly becomes clear that the bottleneck is not the coding itself.

Sometimes it is a review. Sometimes the project status. Sometimes three places where you need to check if the function is really fit for release. Sometimes the question "who even has the context for this PR?" which comes back with every bigger task.

And here comes the temptation: since AI can quickly write code, let's build our own tool. Our own dashboard. Our own triage system. Our own version of what is missing in the existing SaaS.

Tempting. And that's exactly why you have to be careful.

You need a simple filter: when to reach for a ready-made function, when to supplement an existing tool with a thin helper, and when an idea should wait until it becomes part of a fuller team workflow.

Before we build the first solution for a team or company, we need to know why we are building it.

Team friction as a signal

Throughout most of the course, you worked with AI mainly at the level of your own workflow: PRD, roadmap, plan, implementation, tests, review, refactoring, debugging. This is still the perspective of a single person guiding the agent through the project.

In a team, challenges usually look different. They are typically scattered among tools, people, and rituals.

Team friction is a recurring coordination cost: something the team has to decide, check, rewrite, or synchronize so often that it begins to lose momentum or motivation to work.

In practice, it looks familiar. No one knows what has changed since yesterday until they open six tools. The review is stalled because the domain owner hasn't noticed the PR. The status in Linear says one thing, CI shows another, and the release note a third. Customer support has an important signal, but it doesn't reach the backlog in a form that the technical team can work with.

These are not product ideas yet, just signals. A recurring cost does not automatically mean "let's build a tool," but only "something recurring is happening here that may be worth analyzing."

Part of this analysis is the question of whether it is even worth removing this friction. In the fourth module, we distinguished accidental complexity from essential complexity (F. Brooks): some friction is accidental and can indeed be shortened with a tool, but some is essential and results from a real constraint, process, or decision that is not apparent up close. Before you consider something "fixable," make sure you are not looking at friction that has its reason.

In the prework, we talked about agents, harnesses, and prompts as layers of individual work. Now we add a team layer: existing systems, processes, and responsibilities. AI can help connect them, but it doesn't relieve you from deciding whether that connection makes sense.

SaaS is More Than Just Features

The most common mistake when building internal tools looks like this:

"In Linear, Jira, or GitHub, we are missing one feature. Actually, this system is useless. With AI, we can build our own, better version of the system over the weekend."

The problem is that SaaS is much more than just a list of visible features.

SaaS is an application that someone maintains as a platform: with infrastructure, security, permissions, audits, reliability, onboarding, support, billing, availability, and vendor risk.

When you compare "we are missing one view" with "let's write our own tool," it is very easy to equate the visible function with the invisible responsibility. And this is the path to failure.

If you are building a full replacement for a tool, you take over not only the screen you lack. You also take over questions about data access, configuration, logs, API errors, maintaining integration after changes on the provider's side, onboarding new people, and whether the client's data has been exposed too widely.

And suddenly, the "little helper" starts to smell like a full internal product. That's a completely different conversation.

That’s why in this lesson we don’t start with the question “can we build this?”. AI makes the answer increasingly often “yes”. We start with the question “should we take on this responsibility?”.

Buy, replenish, or build

The classic "buy or build" decision is broken down here into three categories, because in the AI era a third, usually the best option emerges: complement.

Buy or use a default function when the problem is generic. A summary of a single ticket, a label suggestion, a view of overdue issues, a basic status report, a CI dashboard. These are things that providers are increasingly solving within the product itself.

Use when the problem arises from locally connecting several systems. GitHub has part of the truth, Linear or Jira has part of the truth, CI has part of the truth, the internal company database has part of the truth, and the team manually pieces it together in their minds every day. Then a thin helper can make sense.

Build a more complete workflow only after the helper has survived contact with reality: someone uses it, it saves real time, has an owner, has a clear scope, and stops being a one-off report.

This middle category, complementing existing systems, is the most important in the fifth module.

The completion consciously does not pretend to be the source system: it does not replace GitHub, Linear, Jira, or CI, but reads signals from them, organizes them, and redirects people to the places where the decision should really be made.

Note the last step. A thin helper does not have to immediately become an application, an agent in the SDK, a pipeline, or an artifact registry. First, it should answer the question: does this problem even deserve permanent support?

Opportunity map for the internal builder

The internal builder in this lesson is not a new job title. It is a developer who can spot local team friction and build the smallest tool to remove that pain, without rebuilding the entire company.

To avoid guessing, we will use a simple artifact: a map of opportunities.

An opportunity map is a brief classification of a single friction signal, a working note, not a product backlog or market analysis. It is meant to protect you from two extremes: "we won’t do anything about it because that’s just how it is" and "let’s build our own system for everything".

You fill it in in four fields:

FieldQuestion
Friction signalWhat repeats often enough to cost the team time or attention?
SaaS / default answerDo existing tools already solve this problem well enough?
Thin helperWhat is the smallest layer that could connect local signals?
First useful versionHow will we check value without turning the helper into a product, logging, and deployment?

See three example classifications.

Morning project status

  • Friction signal: no one knows what has changed since yesterday without opening several tools.
  • SaaS / default response: GitHub notifications, views in Linear/Jira, CI dashboard, and release pages.
  • Thin helper: read-only digest that combines overdue issues, risky PRs, failed CI jobs, lack of owner, and release status.
  • First useful version: static HTML from JSON/CSV exports or API data.

Review without clear criteria

  • Friction signal: review blocks merge because quality criteria are implicit.
  • SaaS / default response: code owners, review requests, status checks, and existing repo rules.
  • Thin helper: auxiliary classification of PR risk and suggestion of reviewers.
  • First useful version: comment from the agent under the PR on several sample PRs.

AI Artifacts Copied Manually

  • Friction signal: the team copies the same prompts, rules, and skills between repositories.
  • SaaS / default response: internal documentation, wiki, or manual copying if the scale is small.
  • Thin helper: a single source of truth for AI artifacts.
  • First usable version: a repository with a test package and manual installation in one project.

In all three examples, the pattern is the same: we don't start with implementation, but with qualifying the problem. Before the agent writes code, we determine whether the problem should even receive code.

There is an important limitation here: you can build anything, but you cannot build everything.

The potential of agents is easy to confuse with unlimited bandwidth. Since a prototype is created faster, it’s tempting to build ten small tools at once. However, you still have limited time, energy, token budget, and attention required to maintain what you build. Every helper that starts working in the team will need fixes, explanations, responses to errors, and decisions on whether it still solves a real problem.

That's why it's better to build 2-3 well-thought-out helpers that truly reduce team friction, rather than 10 prototypes that no one will maintain. With limited time and attention, it's not about how many things you kick off, but whether you accurately choose which problem actually gets code.

After the opportunity map, you choose the next mode of work — and you already know both from previous modules. For a larger, unclear topic (when you don't yet understand stakeholders, data, risks, and maintenance costs) return to the fuller flow /10x-shape -> /10x-prd -> /10x-roadmap. For a narrow signal with a clear first version, go straight to implementation: /10x-new -> /10x-research -> /10x-plan -> /10x-implement, and break down subsequent tasks only when the first version shows real value.

Before you start building, take the cheapest possible step: consult the idea with the manager and preferably also with the team. Often, these are the same people for whom you are building the tool, because you are addressing team friction, but they have a broader context than you and may know why the friction exists in the first place. It often turns out that your view of the problem is incomplete or that you are addressing essential complexity rather than accidental complexity. This conversation takes a few minutes but can save a week of building a tool that no one needed.

Only after the framing passes this initial conversation is it worth validating it more deeply. The opportunity map classifies the problem on paper. Before turning this classification into code, test it on the people you are building for, following the principles of The Mom Test, an approach to user conversations described in Rob Fitzpatrick's book. Its essence is a simple shift: instead of asking for opinions about your idea, you ask for facts from the interviewee's past, because opinions can lie, while concrete situations much less often.

When you already have an idea, an opportunity map, and for bigger topics also shape-notes.md and prd.md, don’t ask people whether they "would use such a tool" or if they "like the idea." Such questions mainly gauge politeness, aspirations, and the desire to support your enthusiasm from your interlocutors. Ask about recent specific situations, current workarounds, the real cost of friction, and tools they have already tried.

In the package of this lesson, you get the skill /10x-mom-test. Its task is not to sell your idea to the interlocutor, but to criticize the assumptions, prepare questions for 1:1 conversations, and create a short survey that will check if you are heading in the right direction.

Example: Collective friction digest

Imagine a typical product company. The team uses GitHub, Linear or Jira, CI, release notes, and an internal database with information about customers, plans, flags, or accounts.

Each of these tools works. None of them is "broken." Yet, the daily status requires manually jumping between contexts.

GitHub shows PRs but does not indicate which ones relate to the enterprise client. Linear or Jira show tickets but do not indicate whether the related PR has failing CI. CI shows failed jobs but does not indicate whether they block an important release. The internal database has domain-specific information that SaaS usually does not see.

This is a perfect candidate for a supplement, not a replacement.

Such a helper could generate a morning digest:

Morning team digest

Risky PRs:
PR #1842 touches billing, has red CI and a ticket from an enterprise customer.
PR #1839 has been waiting 3 days without review; domain owner: Marta.

Blocked tickets:
PAY-412 has status "In Review", but there is no open PR.
AUTH-91 has a merged PR, but the ticket is still "In Progress".

Release:
Release 2026.06.11 has 2 failed jobs and 1 ticket with a customer flag.

Decisions for today:
Ask the owner of PAY-412, check the failed job in the release pipeline, and assign a reviewer to PR #1839.

This is not a full project management system, just a small view: it connects distributed signals and suggests where the team should look first.

AI mainly helps here in two areas. It can summarize chaotic text from issues, PRs, and comments, as well as name patterns: "this looks like a lack of an owner," "this looks like a status mismatch," "this looks like a release risk."

The greatest value does not come from the summary itself, as such features are also provided by vendors. It comes from the local connection of signals that a single SaaS by definition does not see.

GitHub does not know your internal client logic. Jira does not have the full history of failed jobs. CI does not know the context of the conversation in the ticket. The helper combines these signals only when it remains modest: it reads, summarizes, links to sources, and helps people make decisions.

First usable version

The very first helper is a bit boring.

There is no administrative panel, login, deployment, feature backlog, or own database if a file or static report suffices.

The first usable version is the simplest form that allows you to check whether this workflow helps the team.

You can start with a mocked Markdown report based on a few prepared examples. Then you can move on to a local script that reads JSON/CSV exports, static HTML manually uploaded to an internal channel, or a simple digest run by one person. An agent, application, pipeline, or scheduler only appear once the earlier version actually proves helpful.

This order is important because AI lowers the cost of the first prototype, but does not eliminate the maintenance cost. A small script that helped once in a standup does not require a product process. The same script used daily by the team begins to need an owner, error handling, clear behavior when data is missing, and decisions on who can see the results.

There is also a disclaimer about data. If you are working with mocks, local exports, read-only signals, or data without sensitive context, you can start lightly.

If you are handling real company, client, or production data, thinking about access, permissions, and auditability shifts earlier.

This is not scaring with compliance and audits, but simply engineering hygiene.

The leverage of small improvements

It's worth addressing this for a simple reason: in technical teams, reputation is often built around visible improvements rather than presentations about productivity. Around situations where someone says: "this no longer hurts."

If you remove friction that everyone notices, trust in your technical decisions grows. People start to believe that you understand not only the code but also the team's workflow.

This is not a promise of a promotion and there is no magic path of "build a dashboard, receive a raise." Instead, there is a practical leverage: you see a local problem, you classify it responsibly, you build a small layer where it makes sense, and you don’t pretend to have replaced the platform. Many supervisors will appreciate this.

This lesson section is not mandatory, but it is worth reviewing if you want to become an expert.

Buy, supplement, or build without going to extremes

The classic "build vs buy" decision is a bit too flat for internal tools in the AI era. When the cost of a prototype drops, "build" starts to look too attractive.

If an agent can generate a working draft in an hour, it's easy to think that the economics of decision-making have completely changed. They have changed, but only partially: AI lowers the cost of the first version, iteration, and stitching data, but does not eliminate the cost of maintenance, data responsibility, error handling, changes in the provider's API, and explaining to the team why the report sometimes goes silent.

Therefore, alongside the pair "buy or build," we add a third category: complement. Instead of buying a new tool or building a replacement, you leave the source systems where they are and create a thin layer next to them tailored to the local problem.

Invisible work of SaaS

SaaS has an annoying property: you mainly see the screen and features, not the entire effort of maintaining the platform.

In a well-functioning tool, you don't think daily about client or organization (tenant) isolation, audits, backups, permission models, user onboarding, API versioning, support, and reliability. And that's a good thing, because among other things, that's what you pay for.

The problem arises when you compare your small prototype to a single view in a SaaS product. The prototype may look better in a narrow case because it doesn't carry the full burden of the platform. That doesn't mean it won as a system.

The internal helper should therefore remain dependent on the source systems for as long as possible. It links to the PR, ticket, job, and record, but does not pretend to be a new source of truth.

When a helper becomes a product

A one-time report can be loose. A daily report used by the team cannot.

Signs of maturation are visible quite quickly. The team starts planning the day based on the helper's output. An incorrect or missing report causes real confusion. Requests for new data sources, access control, or operation without manual triggering begin to appear.

At such a moment, it's not about immediately rewriting everything. It's about consciously changing the status: from an experiment to a maintained workflow.

That is why the entire fifth module is built as a sequence. First, you qualify the problem. Then you can build an agent, integrate it into the pipeline, distribute artifacts across the team, or run the work asynchronously.
