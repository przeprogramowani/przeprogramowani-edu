From idea to PRD

The first trap in a course project appears before you write the first line of code.

You have an idea. Sometimes even a quite sensible one: an AI-powered flashcard app, a note-taking system, a dashboard for freelancers, an internal tool for a company. It sounds good because at this stage the idea didn't have to answer any uncomfortable questions yet.

And then you dive into the code, add logging, a dashboard, several tables, a statistics view... and after two weeks it turns out that it's still unclear what kind of application this actually is.

Who will use it? What is the first valuable flow? What is left out of the MVP? What do we do when the user doesn't return after a week?

Exactly.

Therefore, the first move in 10xDevs is not implementation. The first move is a /10x-shape session, a conversation in which the Agent enforces precision. After that, /10x-prd records the agreements as a contract.

Two separate skills, two separate artifacts: shape-notes.md as a record of the planning session and prd.md as the input contract for further work.

A well-conducted session forces decisions that are very easy to overlook on your own. The recorded contract ensures that your subsequent prompts will no longer challenge them.

In the prework, you gathered three pieces of this puzzle: criteria for a good course project (prework 4.2), the concept of an Agent as a system operating in a controlled environment (prework 1.2), and the intuition that a prompt for an Agent is a contract (prework 3.2).

Now we combine these three things into the first real workflow.

Before the Agent gets the repository, it receives your idea. And it subjects it to proper verification and structuring.

Why the Agent Should Ask

Product requirements start with natural language. A person describes the problem, omits obvious details, mixes wishes with decisions, and assumes that the other party "knows what it's about".

If you immediately ask the model for a document, it will fill in the gaps with the most likely assumptions. You will get text that looks complete but may contain decisions you never made.

In agent work, this is especially dangerous. The agent can then very consistently implement the wrong assumption. That is why we choose the order: questions first, then the document.

PRD as a contract for the next steps

In the prework, we talked about the agent's prompt as a contract. The PRD (Product Requirements Document) works similarly, but at a higher level.

The task prompt says: "now do this specific thing."

PRD says: "these are the key frameworks for the project in which this thing makes sense."

Without a PRD, subsequent prompts will start to drift. Sometimes you'll ask for logging in, other times for the dashboard, sometimes for reminders, and sometimes for statistics.

Each prompt on its own may sound correct, but the whole will start to resemble a product made up of random whims.

PRD gives the Agent a more stable point of reference:

  • the user and the problem limit imagination,
  • the range limits the growth of the function,
  • non-goals protect against "while we're at it, let's also add...",
  • success criteria help later in writing the plan and acceptance,
  • open questions remind us where we still have uncertainty.

In the next lesson (M1L2) we will look under the hood of the skills and use PRD to choose the tech stack for a specific problem, not based on trends.

In lesson three (M1L3), the PRD along with the chosen stack go to the project's bootstrap. This is the lesson where we turn contracts into concrete files.

The /10x-shape → /10x-prd → stack selection → bootstrap workflow is divided into three lessons, but it is one continuous process. If the first contract is empty, the rest will only be a faster way to deliver bad decisions.

Faster does not always mean better. Sometimes it simply means... faster in the wrong direction.

Two skills, one goal

The workflow consists of two separate skills. It is worth immediately separating their roles.

/10x-shape leads a Socratic session. It asks, probes, catches gaps. It doesn't come up with what you are building for you. It forces you to clearly describe it.

The result is shape-notes.md: a record of the decisions you made.

/10x-prd is the second step. It takes shape-notes.md and rewrites it into a PRD with an established structure, faithfully, without guessing or adding anything. If something is missing in the notes, it writes it directly in the ## Open Questions section so that you can fill in the detected gaps.

The output is prd.md: a contract based on which the Agent will understand what we want to achieve in this project.

Both skills can also work in brownfield mode. If you run /10x-shape in the directory of an existing project (where package.json, Cargo.toml, or another marker is located), the skill will detect this context and suggest switching to a brownfield session. Instead of asking "what are you building from scratch," it asks "what would you like to add/improve in the system?". More about this mode of operation later in the lesson.

Session /10x-shape in practice

Let's take 10xCards, an example application for creating and reviewing flashcards with AI.

The first version of the idea sounds like this:

## 10xCards - MVP

This is not a bad start to thinking about an MVP. But it's still not enough to start implementation.

Who is the user? What does "repetition" mean? Which moment in the application provides real value? Is there any business logic underneath, or is it just another CRUD with a nice description?

With such a description, the temptation is simple to write to the agent: "prepare a PRD for an AI flashcard app". The model will respond coherently, often even professionally. However, such a document will only elegantly wrap the lack of essential specifics.

The /10x-shape session forces a different path.

The agent takes the lead. It guides you through six phases in a fixed order:

In the Vision & problem phase, the Agent asks: what exactly do you want to solve and why. In our example, this is the moment when the "AI flashcard app" must turn into a specific problem for a specific person.

In the Persona & access control phase, it asks: who is using this and what rights do they have. "User" is not enough. The agent will push you to "an adult learner who chooses materials on their own and wants to turn the read text into trusted flashcards after the study session."

In the MVP discipline phase, scope verification takes place. If your first workflow requires more than three weeks of overtime work, the Agent will not forbid it. But it will lay out the cost and ask for conscious confirmation that you are taking on a longer timeline.

In the Functional Requirements (FRs) + user stories and Business logic + data phases, requirements break down into specific decisions. The agent activates two mechanisms here, which are easy to overlook when thinking independently:

  • Socratic challenge for each FR — for each requirement asks: "what would have to be true for this FR to be wrong?". This tests whether you can defend your own decision and whether you have thought through its implications.
  • Detecting an empty CRUD — if your business logic is "the user adds flashcards and reviews them," the Agent will tell you outright that you're building an empty CRUD. It will enforce a specific domain rule: generating flashcards from pasted text, an acceptance/rejection gateway before saving, a review algorithm. Something that truly turns data into value.

At the end of the session, the Agent goes through a closing soft-gate, verifying the accumulated decisions with six control questions:

  1. Access control — do you know who has access to what?
  2. Data model — do you know what data you need?
  3. Business logic — do you have a one-sentence business rule?
  4. Project artifacts — do you have defined work artifacts?
  5. MVP-in-three-weeks — will the first flow fit into three weeks of after-hours work?
  6. Non-goals — do you know what you are not building?

If there are any gaps anywhere, AI will point out where you still lack answers.

Your role throughout the entire session is to answer concretely, including when the honest response is "I don't know".

PRD Generation: /10x-prd

The second step is to generate a PRD from the notes:

/10x-prd @context/foundation/shape-notes.md

/10x-prd by default reads context/foundation/shape-notes.md and creates the file context/foundation/prd.md. It does one thing: it saves your decisions in a fixed structure so that subsequent agent steps have a source to read from.

PRD describes the product and business, not the technology. Inside you will find the vision, persona, success criteria, user stories, functional requirements, business rules, data model, access control, explicit non-goals, and a list of open questions.

The tech stack, test plan, and deployment are intentionally not in the PRD. They will be included in further skills that consume the PRD as input.

Warning about Phantom PRD

There is one situation where /10x-prd will give you a clear signal that something is wrong - when shape-notes.md lacks details.

The skill checks the notes for four signals: checkpoint, requirements in the FR-NNN format, user stories with the Given/When/Then structure, business rule.

If too many are missing, you receive a warning with specific deficiencies and a suggestion to return to /10x-shape. If you thoroughly go through the planning session with /10x-shape, this should not happen.

Check after generating the PRD

Open prd.md and ask yourself:

  • Do you see a specific user, not a "developer in general"?
  • Is it possible to complete the first workflow from start to finish within three weeks of working after hours (the reality of working on a course project)?
  • Is business logic a specific rule, not "user adds records"?
  • Are there explicit non-goals, or is it just "we leave it for later"?
  • Is ## Open Questions empty or does it contain only truly open issues?

If any answer worries you, edit the document or return to /10x-shape. It is important that this foundation is solid - we will build the entire project on it.

Not only at the start of the project

This workflow is not an initiation ritual (well, maybe a little!). It should become a new habit when starting larger initiatives.

Often people run skills like /10x-shape once, at the start of the project, and then return to the old mode: straight to the code. A week later, they add a new module, integration, or a major flow change, and the Agent has no contract for those changes.

When you add a new module in two weeks, create a mini-version of the same session. Sometimes a short shape for a specific function is enough instead of a full PRD.

The rule remains: before the Agent starts editing files, they must know what problem we are solving and how we will recognize that the work is finished.

Brownfield: session on an existing project

Not every student starts from scratch. If your course project is a change in an existing system (a new module in a side project, expanding a tool from work, refactoring a key flow), the shape/prd workflow works the same, but /10x-shape switches to brownfield mode.

In practice: you run /10x-shape in the directory of an existing project. The skill detects markers (package.json, tsconfig.json, Cargo.toml, go.mod, etc.), suggests brownfield mode, and waits for your confirmation. You can also manually switch the mode if the auto-detection misses.

The same six phases, but the questions shift from "what are you building from scratch" to "what are you changing in what you already have":

  • Vision & problem — what exists today, what hurts and why now (instead of "who has a problem out of nowhere").
  • Persona & access control — what the current authentication looks like and who has which roles (instead of designing auth from scratch).
  • MVP discipline — what is the smallest change that will prove improvement, and what is its blast radius. Instead of "MVP-in-three-weeks" from an empty project: a delta that can be delivered and verified.
  • FRs & user stories — requirements categorized as new, modified, or maintained (instead of treating everything as new).
  • Business logic & data — does this change add a new domain rule, modify an existing one, or is it an infrastructural change.
  • Product framing — whether the product type, scale, or constraints change (yes/no gates instead of full classification from scratch).

At the end of the session, shape-notes.md contains two sections that greenfield does not have: ## Current System (description of what exists) and ## Constraints & Preserved Behavior (what must remain unchanged).

Brownfield PRD

Key difference: a greenfield PRD describes the entire product from scratch. A brownfield PRD describes the delta: what exists today, what changes, what must remain.

When expanding an existing project, in the following lessons you will assess your stack (instead of choosing a new one), and in the lesson about bootstrap you will do a health-check of the project (instead of setting it up from scratch). The Brownfield PRD will be the input contract for these steps, just as the greenfield PRD is the input contract for selecting the technical stack and bootstrap.

How to use 10x-cli?

Before you move on to practice and run /10x-shape, you need the course 10x-cli: a tool that provides skills and materials for your project.

We invite you to watch an instructional video on how to use the tool, below you will also find a textual guide. Up-to-date information about supported commands can always be found in the project's README.md:

You don't need to install it globally. You run it via npx with the @latest tag to always have the newest version:

npx @przeprogramowani/10x-cli@latest auth

auth will ask you for the email address associated with your account on the Circle platform. Then it will send a magic link to that address. You click the link, the CLI saves the token locally, and from that moment you have access to the materials. This is a one-time step; you don't need to repeat it for every lesson.

The second step is to download the skill package for this lesson:

npx @przeprogramowani/10x-cli@latest get m1l1

There will be a summary of the whole lesson here soon on a separate page.

The CLI fetches skills from the server and saves them in your project. For Claude Code, they go to .claude/skills/, for Cursor to .cursor/skills/, and for Copilot to .github/skills/.

Each lesson has its own bundle and m1l1 is the tag for the first lesson in the first module. In the next lesson, it will be m1l2 and so on.

In the package for this lesson, you will get /10x-init, /10x-shape, and /10x-prd, which we will use shortly.

Rerunning the same get m1l1 command is safe: the CLI checks what you already have and updates only what has changed.

The CLI also provides helper skills that teach your agent how to work with this tool. At the start, the most important is 10x-cli-setup, a skill that will guide you (or rather your agent) through the installation, authentication, and configuration of the CLI for the chosen AI tool. If something doesn't work with auth or get, the agent with this skill can diagnose the problem and lead you to a solution.

Install it via npx skills:

npx skills add przeprogramowani/10x-cli

If you want the helper skills to be available in all your projects, add the -g flag:

npx skills add przeprogramowani/10x-cli -g

Alternatively, if you are not using npx skills, you can download SKILL.md directly from the GitHub repository and place it manually in your agent's skills directory (e.g. .claude/skills/10x-cli-setup/SKILL.md).

The package also includes 10x-cli-guide - a daily use skill that teaches the agent how to use 10xCLI: downloading packages, listing modules, switching tool profiles, error diagnostics

After finishing the work, you should have two files in context/foundation/: shape-notes.md from the /10x-shape session and prd.md generated by /10x-prd.

Which models and tools to choose

To run /10x-shape, you need an AI agent with access to a language model. There are over a hundred models and a dozen tools on the market. Below are specific recommendations instead of a full market overview.

The simplest path: subscription

Buy a subscription from Anthropic or OpenAI and start working.

For $20/month you get solid models for everyday work: Claude Pro (Sonnet 4.6) on the Anthropic side, ChatGPT Plus on the OpenAI side. It's enough for the /10x-shape session, generating PRD, and most tasks from Module 1.

For $100/month you get top models with higher limits: Claude Max (from Opus) on the Anthropic side, ChatGPT Pro on the OpenAI side. It makes sense if you want to work extensively with these tools and care about the quality of reasoning without worrying about API bills and limits.

There is one more argument for subscription that is not obvious at first: when actively coding with AI, API costs accumulate quickly. One /10x-shape session is tens of thousands of tokens. A full day of work with an agent amounts to millions of tokens.

Even with cheap Chinese models, the monthly bill can exceed $20–50 faster than you think. And with premium models, just a few intense days are enough for a $100 subscription to turn out to be cheaper than paying for tokens.

Predictable cost and no token counting are value in themselves when you want to focus on your work.

What do we use?

Throughout the course, we work with Claude Max 5x ($100/month) using the Opus 4.6 model in the variant with a one-million token context window. This is my daily choice and recommendation for people who want a single tool for everything.

Why 4.6 and not the newer Opus 4.7? In practice, 4.7 consumes significantly more tokens while delivering comparable quality in analytical tasks: planning, shape sessions, code review. Why overpay?

Architects and Implementers

Not all models are suitable for the same tasks.

We tested 22 models on the coding benchmark: a full feature implementation with a database, frontend, and tests. The results? The budget DeepSeek V4 Flash (82.5 points, cost $0.02 per task) beat the premium Opus 4.7 (76.8 points, cost $8.69). 180 times cheaper, higher score.

But this is an implementation benchmark. In analytical tasks, such as conducting a planning session or reasoning about architecture, top models have an advantage that a coding benchmark does not capture.

Hence the division, which will be the main theme of the course:

  • Architects / thinkers — Opus, Sonnet, Gemini Pro, GPT. Analysis, planning, multi-step reasoning. More expensive, but in tasks requiring thinking, they have no reasonable substitutes.
  • Implementers — DeepSeek V4 Flash, Qwen 3.6 Plus, Qwen3 Coder, MiniMax M2.7. Excellent price-to-code quality ratio. Some of them have free tiers on OpenRouter.

Alternative: OpenRouter + OpenCode

You don't need to buy a subscription. OpenRouter provides access to over a hundred models with a single API key. You pay for usage, not for the month. You register, top up credits (or use free tiers), and have access to all the models from the table above.

OpenCode is an open-source CLI agent with over 160 thousand stars on GitHub. It works with any model provider: OpenRouter, Anthropic, OpenAI, local models via Ollama.

Our encoding benchmark (22 models) was conducted precisely on OpenCode with models through OpenRouter. These results are not theoretical.

This path requires more setup at the start, but gives you full control over costs and access to Chinese models that dominated our benchmark. If your budget is limited, you want to experiment, or care about privacy (OpenCode does not store your code), it is worth considering this option.

Test on your own tasks

Our data is one benchmark, one task. Your project and your preferences may yield different results.

Try 2–3 models on your own project before deciding permanently. Check how the model handles /10x-shape on your specific idea. This is the best test you can perform.
