In the previous lesson on Chatbot vs Agent vs Harness, we set up a simple model: when working with AI, you no longer evaluate just the model, but the entire workflow system around it.

If you arrived here from the quiz and haven't read that material, one sentence is enough for you: the agent may deliver the result faster than you, but that still doesn't mean you have learned anything.

This is exactly where it's easiest to deceive yourself. The code works, the diff looks reasonable, the ticket moves off the board, and you have the feeling that you’re "getting into a new area faster than ever." Sounds good. Sometimes too good.

Then comes the review or a conversation with someone more experienced. The question arises, why did you choose Promise.all, where does the SSR behavior come from, or why does this handler catch the exception exactly at this place. And suddenly the classic emerges: the solution exists, but you can't defend it.

This is the most important failure mode of this lesson: approve without defense. The agent delivered something, you clicked approval, but no problem model was formed in your mind.

Fast does not mean deep

At the beginning of 2026, a randomized study by Anthropic was published on 52 programmers learning a previously unknown Python library, Trio.

The result was inconvenient. The group with access to AI performed significantly worse on questions testing comprehension, code reading, and debugging, and on average did not gain much time in the process. In practice, the most frequently cited conclusion from this study is this: a task delivered faster does not mean faster competency building.

The biggest difference appeared during debugging. It makes sense. A person without AI more often has to encounter the error themselves, read it, misunderstand it, fix it, and only then piece the mechanism together in their mind. A person with AI can skip this entire stage. Convenient, but expensive.

This is not an argument for going back to the documentation and pretending the tools have disappeared. That would be romanticism, not strategy.

This is an argument for separating two things: productivity in the task and skill development. Sometimes they go together. Sometimes they don't. And if you don’t separate them, you can easily confuse one with the other.

A similar signal appears in the MIT Media Lab project Your Brain on ChatGPT. This study concerned essay writing, not programming, and the authors themselves note that it is still a preliminary, non-peer-reviewed stage. Despite this, the trend is familiar: when the model takes over too much of the cognitive work, comfort increases, but engagement and sense of ownership over the result decrease.

Ethan Mollick describes this mechanism very accurately: the problem is not the use of AI itself, but the default mode of "give me a ready-made answer." Such a mode gives you a signal of progress. It does not always provide progress.

Why AI Can Ruin Learning

The negative impact of AI on learning does not come from some kind of "model magic." The mechanism is more down-to-earth.

When the agent gives you a ready-made solution too early, it skips several stages where you would normally build understanding:

  • You do not formulate your own hypothesis. Without your own attempt to solve it, it is harder to notice what you still do not understand.
  • You do not go through the friction of error. Debugging is painful, but it is what forces you to build a working model in your head.
  • You are not engaging in active playback. Reading the answers gives a sense of familiarity with the topic but does not check whether you can independently apply this knowledge.
  • You quickly get a sense of closure. Since the code works, the brain easily considers the topic "done," even though the understanding is superficial.

That's why the delegation mode so often ends up weaker than it seems. AI shortens the path to the result, but in the process it can cut out the parts of the process that are most valuable in learning.

The same tools, two different results

The most interesting thing about the Anthropic study is not even that some people learned worse with AI. The most interesting thing is that others learned very well with it.

The strongest pattern was called Generation-then-Comprehension. First generating a working solution, then inquiring about the logic, consequences, alternatives, and trade-offs. This pattern was one of the best in the entire experiment.

This is a difference worth remembering permanently: AI as a generator and AI as a tutor are not two similar working styles. These are two different workflows.

In the first one you ask: "do it for me".

In the second, you ask: "help me understand what has just been created and how to use it next time".

And it is this second mode that we want to set for you here as the default. Because shortly, in the lesson Agent prompt as a contract, you will see how to formulate better commands for the agent, and in Context that wears out you will see what happens to the quality of work when the conversation lasts too long. Before you get there, you need one more thing: your own learning mode.

Three practices that make a difference

1. Build, then take apart

The worst thing you can do in a new area is to stop at the level of "it works, so let's move on".

The better move looks like this:

  1. You ask AI for help in building something that works.
  2. Then you switch to analysis mode.
  3. You don't just ask "what does this do?", but above all "why exactly like this?" and "what would break if I changed this element?".

This is the moment when the generated artifact starts working for your development. You already have the code, so you don't have to struggle simultaneously with a blank page and a new concept. You can focus on understanding.

Why does this work? Because this practice restores two elements that the delegation mode usually cuts out: elaboration and checking understanding. You no longer just consume a ready-made solution. You have to name the logic, compare alternatives, and anticipate the consequences of a change. This is exactly the kind of cognitive effort that builds a lasting working model.

A mini-prompt worth copying:

I'm learning a new area. Explain what has just been created,
step by step, one concept at a time.

After each step, ask me a short comprehension question.
Do not proceed until I answer correctly.

Focus on "why this way", "what are the trade-offs"
and "when this approach would be a bad choice".

This one added request makes a huge difference. Instead of a wall of text, you get a learning rhythm in which you have to reproduce something in your own words. That's the point.

The failure mode of this practice is simple: you read the explanation, nod your head, feel like you understand... and after two days, nothing sticks. If there was no checking question or your own reproduction of the logic, it is very likely that you did not understand at all. You just read it.

2. Close the memory, not just the session

Even a good conversation with AI does not stay in your mind by itself. It's not Netflix, which "somehow gets saved". Unfortunately.

If the session was valuable, immediately turn it into material for active playback. The simplest way: generate flashcards.

Based on our conversation, generate Q/A flashcards.
Focus on key concepts, decisions, and error signals,
not on syntax details.

Each answer: maximum 2-3 sentences.
Add questions that test knowledge application,
not just definitions.

Then you put it into Anki or another spaced repetition system and return to it for a few minutes each day. A meta-analysis by Adesope and colleagues shows that practice testing alone provides a very strong increase in retention compared to passive review of notes.

This is not a "cool add-on for the ambitious." This is the moment when knowledge stops being a one-time conversation and starts being your asset.

Why does it work? Because memory is not mainly strengthened by rereading, but by trying to retrieve information from your mind. Flashcards and control questions force you to actively recall the concept, not just recognize it visually. It's a big difference.

3. Set the learning mode as default

Discipline is unreliable. Especially when the deadline is pressing under your feet.

Therefore, it is worth shifting some of the responsibility from the habit to the configuration. If your tool can act more like a tutor than a generator, set it up that way in advance.

You can do this with your own system prompt or your own instructions, for example like this:

Act like a programming tutor.

Explain briefly, but to the point.
Show 2 perspectives or 2 sensible alternatives.
After each important step, ask a checking question.
If I answer incorrectly, point out the mistake and ask a simpler question.
Do not end with a ready-made answer if the topic is new.

If you don't want to build this from scratch, some tools already have modes closer to learning than pure generation. Claude Code today has output styles Explanatory and Learning, set via /config. In ChatGPT, there is Study Mode, designed specifically for step-by-step work and control questions.

However, it is not about a specific brand. It is about the selection criteria:

  • whether the tool guides you step-by-step instead of just giving you a ready-made solution,
  • does it ask checking questions,
  • whether it helps compare alternatives and trade-offs,
  • is it easy to switch from "delivering the task" to "understanding the topic".

If the answer is "no," even a very good model will more often do the work for you than teach you.

Why does it work? Because you no longer try to rely solely on your own discipline. You change the default behavior of the system so that it more often leads you towards questions, explanations, and checking understanding, and less often towards a quick ready-made solution. In other words: you reduce the chance that under time pressure you will automatically fall into the worst mode of work.

Work like this from tomorrow

  • Decision rule: if you are learning a new area, do not end the interaction at the generated solution. Treat generation as a start, not a finale.
  • Worth remembering: before considering the topic closed, answer without hints one checking question about logic, trade-off, or error signal.
  • Action: set one fixed learning mode that enforces step-by-step explanations and control questions. Preferably today, not "sometime".

This is a concrete change in your daily workflow. You no longer just ask "how to deliver the result as quickly as possible?". You also ask: "what will remain in me after this session?".

And this is how you will know whether AI is working for your development or just for your short-term convenience.
