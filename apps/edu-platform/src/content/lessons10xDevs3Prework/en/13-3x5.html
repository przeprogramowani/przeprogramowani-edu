In the previous lesson, you decided which language to speak with the agent. The final operational decision before starting M1 is the choice of the model.

You open Cursor, Claude Code, Codexa, or Copilot and see dozens of names. GPT-5.5, GPT-5.4 mini, GPT-5.3-Codex, Claude Opus 4.7, Sonnet 4.6, Gemini 3.1 Pro, DeepSeek V4, Qwen, Kimi, GLM...

Trying to find your way in this cornucopia crisis, you will quickly find various rankings that promise the objective truth about the "best" model.

Usually people choose the one with the highest position in the SWE-bench and start coding, because that’s probably... how it should be done?

It shouldn't. In 2026, hunting for the highest ranking score is the worst way to choose a model. Better: match the model's rank to the task, set sensible default project settings, and learn to distinguish signal from noise in information about new versions.

What to choose at the beginning

Before explaining why rankings are not worth trusting, let's start with the concrete — what to choose at the beginning of working with tools so that you can start programming with AI.

Paul Gauthier from Aider proposed in 2024 a role division that became the standard for working with agents in 2026: Coder for quick execution in familiar tasks and Architect for planning, analysis, and decision-making under uncertainty. Translating this into specific models:

Practically: by default, set the model that reliably delivers daily execution. Leave the strongest model for implementation plans, architecture, difficult debugging, code review, and decisions you don’t want to revise five times.

This heuristic is tool-independent. Don’t skimp on the model when success depends on architecture, migration, security, UI evaluation, coordination of multiple files, or work that needs to go into production without many rounds of manual correction.

If you have a more expensive Claude Code or Codex subscription (for $100-$200 per month), optimizing cost per prompt is not as urgent. In that case, simplicity often wins: you can use the same top-tier model for both planning and execution.

One caveat: these names have a shelf life shorter than this prework. Models change every few weeks.

The course model update channel on Circle will be your source of truth during 10xDevs. The division into tiers (light / daily / planning) remains stable regardless of the names.

Cheap models from China

The biggest change in recent months is not that frontier models have become a bit better. It is that open-source models from China have started to offer "good enough" quality at prices that can seem absurdly low compared to Western premium models.

DeepSeek V4 Flash, Qwen by Alibaba Cloud Model Studio, Kimi K2.6/K2.5 and GLM by Z.ai or aggregated plans like Alibaba Coding Plan are real options for daily work: fixing simple errors, generating tests, exploring repos, rewriting small components, working with documentation, and parallel subtasks.

Sounds like a free lunch? Well, not exactly.

A cheap model can be the most expensive choice if, due to weaker fit to your repo, it generates three rounds of corrections, loses context, mixes conventions, or requires heavier review. In that case, you save on tokens but pay with time, attention, and risk of regression.

Therefore, treat Chinese models as a budget working layer, not an automatic frontier replacement:

  • check the data policy, processing region, and company consent,
  • use them first for reversible and well-testable tasks,
  • do not leave them responsible for architecture planning, security, or high-risk product decisions without a stronger model or senior review,
  • follow OpenRouter and official price lists, because availability and prices change faster than names in the dropdown.

And one more thing: at this moment, we do not recommend using these models locally (instead of via API) as the main tool for coding. The best small models are still much weaker than models available in the cloud. Models that provide satisfactory quality locally, such as Qwen3.6-27B, still require a powerful workstation, a large amount of VRAM, and often a hardware budget exceeding 30 thousand PLN if you want to work comfortably (without long waiting times for the response to be generated and without overheating hardware).

This is a great topic for experiments, but a poor default choice for the start of the course.

During 10xDevs we will return to this in practice: we will show how to evaluate the model not by name, but by the quality of the diff, the loop cost, and the fit to the harness.

You have something to set. But why not just choose the one with the highest SWE-bench? Because that result is misleading — let's see how.

Why rankings lie

A benchmark is three things at once: a set of tasks, a metric, and an evaluation protocol. Change any element — the result will change. The sentence "Model X scores 80% on SWE-bench" does not tell you which benchmark variant, which harness, which step limit, or which metric.

In daily work, rankings can mislead you in three specific ways.

Goodhart's Law: when a benchmark becomes a target, producers optimize for it instead of the actual capabilities of the model. The result? In February 2026, OpenAI stopped reporting SWE-bench Verified results — citing saturation and data contamination. Specific leading models were able to reproduce benchmark solutions from memory.

The benchmark, which was supposed to measure coding quality, started measuring the model's ability to memorize tests.

The benchmark measures not what you think: FeatureBench showed that Claude Opus 4.5 achieves 74% on bug fixes, but only 11% on building new features. For two years, the industry equated "can fix bugs" with "can code" because SWE-bench was the only popular benchmark.

This is the most dangerous failure mode of the ranking — it gives the illusion of having data that you do not have.

pass@1 overestimates reliability: if the model solves a task with a 90% probability in one step (pass@1 = 0.9), then in five consecutive steps it will complete it correctly in only 59% of cases (0.9⁵ = 0.59). The longer the agent's loop, the greater the discrepancies. In production, pass^k matters — whether the model can succeed every time, not just at least once.

Each benchmark goes through a lifecycle lasting 12-18 months. HumanEval is saturated until 2024. SWE-bench Verified withdrawn in February 2026.

The benchmark you trust today will stop differentiating models within a year.

StatusExamplesWhat it means

In other words: never rely on a single benchmark. Choose based on several and include cost per token (or ideally the solution) in comparisons, not just the percentage in the ranking.

The token price, request price, and subscription price are just the beginning. The real cost includes the number of iterations, review quality, plan accuracy, time to merge, and whether the model finishes the job in one go or leaves you with "almost works".

When we built our own benchmark

From exactly this frustration, 10x-bench was born — our internal, open source benchmark. Public rankings test fixing bugs in Python repositories. But if your team delivers sites in Astro, React, and Tailwind, those numbers tell you almost nothing.

10x-bench tests something different: whether the model can build a complete, ready-to-deploy multi-page website from a single prompt — without iterative corrections. 10 criteria (7 automatic + 3 evaluated by humans), a strict input condition: if npm run build fails, the result is zero — just like in production.

The most interesting thing turned out to be what no synthetic benchmark checks. Models that flawlessly generated the code structure simultaneously made up podcast episodes, inserted "rickroll" YouTube IDs, and fabricated band biographies. 74 attempts, 14 models — these content failures turned out to be the most common mode of failure.

The entire project is open source on 10xbench.ai. A broader look at the topic of benchmarks — five structural problems, lifecycle, weighted decision card — can be found in the ebook AI Model Rankings Lie.

The detailed report from our benchmark will be shown in the first lesson of the course. In the prework, one rule is enough for you: a benchmark is the beginning of a conversation about the model, not the end of the decision.

How to Stay Updated Without FOMO

Since rankings lie, how to even keep track of when to change the model? The answer doesn't require daily scrolling through X.

Start with the signal hierarchy — from the strongest:

  1. Official labs blogs (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind) — when a new model appears, you will learn about it from the first source.
  2. OpenRouter and usage rankings (OpenRouter, Chatbot Arena) — do not say which model is "better," but show availability, prices, popularity, and actual traffic between models. It's a good radar, especially for models outside the main dropdown of your tool.
  3. Synthetic benchmarks (SWE-bench Pro, SWE-rebench) — treat as one of many data points, not as the final answer.

Instead of daily monitoring, set yourself a biweekly model check: every two weeks, check if the tools you use have new models or if the cost multipliers have changed. If not — continue working with what you have.

As a 10xDevs participant, you have an additional advantage — the course channel on Circle acts as a filter, allowing you to receive information about changes in models processed and with context, instead of having to sift through the noise on your own.

What is worth knowing

  • Decision rule: start with the main models in the tools you use on a daily basis, and treat Chinese alternatives as a conscious cost optimization for selected tasks.
  • It is worth remembering: before using DeepSeek, Qwen, Kimi, or GLM in work with the company's repository, check the data policy, processing region, and client rules. Price does not override compliance. Unfortunately.
  • Action: set three default options in your preferred tool: a model for daily execution, a cheap/light model for simple tasks, and a powerful model for implementation planning.
