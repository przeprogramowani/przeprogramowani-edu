In this module, we have touched on the topic of AI and legacy from two perspectives – scaling context and scanning the project for candidates for further refactoring. You have learned about additional CLI tools that help save valuable context windows, as well as examples of combining long-known tools like Git with the vast potential of agents.

In this lesson, we will go in a different direction.

We will try to find a way to ensure that a successful project, which grows with every change-id, can be maintained in a week, a month, or even a quarter.

In this lesson, we want to look towards the place where everything actually begins. Towards business and the real world, where our application is grounded.

And here AI comes into play as a business domain modeling coach, which means mapping a certain piece of reality in our code – as coherently and precisely as possible. Drawing from the best practices of the Domain-Driven Design (DDD) approach, we can use an agent to explore the vocabulary used by experts in a given area, and then transfer it to code. We can examine the strategic assumptions from the prd and check whether our code actually supports them and protects against breaking them, or even encourages breaking them. We can search for and classify subdomains, considering what truly determines the success of the project and what is a side issue – something that can be delegated or implemented through libraries.

Before we begin: this lesson is not a DDD course. We will not delve into five-layer aggregates or the repository debate. We take from Domain-Driven Design exactly what is needed to extract sensible opportunities from legacy for the "post-MVP" stage. We are looking for ways to optimize code to make further project scaling easier and more predictable.

From "refactor code" to "discover the domain"

Refactoring from the previous lesson answered the question: how to safely change this code? DDD answers a different question: does this code reflect how the business operates?

This is not cosmetic. If you confuse these two questions, you will get a plan from the Agent that sounds credible, but perpetuates the mess because it designs around names that are themselves a mistake.

Domain-Driven Design gave us a vocabulary that has been used for over two decades: ubiquitous language, bounded contexts, invariants, aggregates. Classics, books by Evans and Vernon. The problem with this classic approach has always been the same: the entry cost is high. It's best to start with an interview with a domain expert, and there may not be any around us – after all, not every street is walked by flashcard and SRS algorithm creators. It would also be good to conduct one or two workshops so that everyone in the project starts using the same terminology. It would also be good to map our current understanding of the domain or process to how it actually works and where the unknowns lie. Quite a bit of work for a refactoring that the client might not even notice.

LLMs reduce the cost of this type of architecture optimization almost to zero (or at least to a "good enough" level). The largest language models have gathered hundreds of concepts and terms from various business areas during training - banking, logistics, or economics. The models can explain, differentiate, and check if a given name in your code means what it should.

Moreover, agents equipped with tools are also excellent workshop facilitators. You set initial conditions, game rules, provide the interface, and conduct Event Storming. Solo, with an agent, again at a "good enough" level.

This is a benefit that has so far been practically unavailable: AI combines the classic domain modeling with the semantic potential of the model and turns things "for architects after training" into something you can do solo, after hours, on someone else's repository.

In this lesson, we will go through several steps inspired by DDD that will help you design the next stage of your project and elevate its architecture to a higher level.

First language: two ways to ubiquitous language

There is a principle from the DDD canon that sounds trivial but is the foundation of further analysis: linguistic boundaries reveal context boundaries. Where the same term starts to mean something different, there runs a seam between two areas of the system.

In the fintech project, the word Account means simultaneously:

  1. User accountAccount as login, email, password, profile, 2FA.

  2. Bank accountAccount as IBAN, balance, currency, transaction history.

  3. Accounting accountAccount as an entry in the chart of accounts, e.g., 201 — settlements with customers.

Problem: in the code you have classes like AccountService, AccountStatus, AccountClosed, but it is unclear whether you are closing a user profile, a bank account, or an accounting account.

In reality, "3xAccount" should be broken down into completely different terms from various contexts:

1) UserProfile
2) BankAccount
3) LedgerAccount

IMAGE — https://images.przeprogramowani.pl/images/2026/06/LoYlavaZDuCJnsEP_fTEuAY.jpeg

In such a structure, no one is surprised that we "calculate the current state of BankAccount", and that the "UserProfile has a missing email".

That's why we start with the language. It's the cheapest move that most often immediately hits the core of the problem with how our project looks from the inside.

I will show you this with an example that will run throughout the entire lesson: asynchronous flashcard generation in 10xCards, a project you know from previous modules. Take a look at what happens with a single concept.

In the product document (PRD), there is a “generation session”, an entity with a lifecycle, a number of ordered cards, and a finalization moment. In the code we built preparing for the lesson, this entity does not exist.

There is only a loose identifier appended to the rows with suggestions. And those suggestions? In the database they are called draft, in the interface "suggestions", in the model's response cards, and in team discussions "candidates". One entity, four names.

This is not nitpicking. In conditions of rapid project scaling, where every working day is priceless, and every token burned by the agent is worth double gold, you need to ensure precision. What we do, in what context, on which elements of the system. This is the essence of ubiquitous language from DDD.

How to extract such a system dictionary using an agent? In two ways.

Mode one: interview with a domain expert

Throughout the entire 10xDevs, we repeated: treat AI like an agent, not a chatbot. In this one section, we make an exception and stay at the level of a regular conversation.

To get to know the language of the chosen domain, we don’t need too many tools. We simply need the largest available language model that has knowledge about the business domain related to our project.

Domain expert: in DDD, this is a person who understands the specific business area for which the system is being built very well, and helps the technical team grasp the true rules, concepts, processes, and exceptions of this domain. They do not have to be a programmer — they can be an accountant, a doctor, a warehouse operator, a credit analyst, a teacher, or a specialist in SRS reviews.

A simulated interview with an expert can be used at the very beginning of a project or during refactoring when we want to obtain a consistent glossary of terms and definitions for everything that can potentially happen in our system – who performs actions, what actions they are, on what data, under what conditions, what is possible and what is not.

We start with a simple filter question to the selected AI (this is a conversation with ChatGPT):

Q: What are flashcards?

A: Flashcards are small study cards, typically used for memorizing concepts, vocabulary, definitions, formulas, or facts.

Q: And what is an SRS?

A: SRS stands for Spaced Repetition System, a system of reviews at spaced intervals. In practice: you learn something on a flashcard, and the system decides when to show it to you again.

It is evident that the model behind this solution understands the world in which our application operates. Let's proceed further.

Q: Let's assume I am building an application for creating and managing flashcards. What are the 3 key business processes that determine the user's perception of such an application? What do I need to know or take care of to ensure this tool provides real value?

A: In a flashcard app, the user rarely evaluates the value based solely on the "creation of cards." The perception of the product mainly depends on whether the app helps them regularly return to the right things, feel progress, and not drown in the chaos of the material.

Our virtual domain expert can be an excellent source of requirements and consultations for unclear processes. As technical people, we primarily focus on tools, languages, and frameworks, and an agent clarifying for us "how it actually works" can offer critically important hints.

And at different levels of abstraction. Even those directly mapping to code.

Q: What are the 3 most commonly chosen algorithms for spaced repetition? How do they differ? Present the results in a table and describe the trade-offs involved in choosing one of them.

A: Leitner is more of a card organization mechanic than a memory model. The user can easily understand: "this card is in box 1, so I review it often; this one is in box 5, so I review it rarely." This is good for simplicity, but weak for precision.

SM-2 already introduces the concept of an individual card rhythm: the card has an interval, answer history, and an ease factor. The original SM-2 comes from SuperMemo; Anki used for years a variant inspired by SM-2, with its own changes, including a different number of answer buttons.

FSRS goes a step further: instead of just shifting intervals according to simple rules, it tries to model memory more directly. FSRS implementations describe it as a model based on variables such as Difficulty, Stability, and Retrievability, meaning difficulty, memory stability, and recall probability.

We can explore algorithms, the rules governing the world, and the behind-the-scenes elements of processes that seem obvious (just check how the process of a package traveling from sender to recipient looks).

And approaching the actual code itself – the expert can finally define the concepts that occur particularly often in a given domain. This is a reference to the naming of classes, methods, and fields in the code. Here, we rely on a property of LLMs, which memorize these frequently occurring concepts:

Q: I care about consistent terminology that reflects reality. Provide examples of 5 non-obvious terms from this domain that an unaware developer might name completely differently, leading to errors in team communication and system implementation.

A: 1) Review (not Test, Quiz, Practice, Attempt)

This type of conversation can be conducted as long as it takes to get to know a domain at an acceptable level. You can turn this into a context file that will serve as a reference in research, planning, or implementation of subsequent functionalities. The agent allows you to name things properly and avoid the "3xAccount" problem.

From manual chat to automated interview

If you want to easily check how a given model handles a domain of your choice, you can run a conversation between two models: one plays the programmer, who asks about details, the other the domain expert, who responds.

Our little tool agent-forum is for this purpose.

The mechanics are simple. In the index.ts file, you define two agents (model plus personality), the number of turns, and the starting topic. An optional summarizer at the end distills the entire conversation into a ready domain brief: glossary of terms, rules, pitfalls.

new Forum({
  threadName: "spaced-repetition-interview",
  rounds: 3,
  agents: [
    { agentId: "beginner-developer", model: orModel("openai/gpt-4o-mini"), personality: BEGINNER_DEVELOPER() },
    { agentId: "spaced-repetition-expert", model: orModel("anthropic/claude-sonnet-4.6"), personality: SPACED_REPETITION_EXPERT() },
  ],
  summarizer: { agentId: "learning-insights", model: orModel("openai/gpt-4o"), personality: LEARNING_INSIGHTS },
});

Running it involves three steps: clone the repository (link above), set OPENROUTER_API_KEY in the .env file (one key provides access to models from multiple providers via OpenRouter), and run npm start. In the terminal, you will observe the conversation in real time.

Each statement from one of the parties goes to a separate markdown file in the threads/ directory, and at the end there is a summary.md, which is the same ubiquitous language dictionary, only compiled automatically from the entire conversation (or any other summary - you decide).

It's still the same idea as above. You ask, the model answers. The difference is that the questions are asked by a second agent, and you receive a ready transcript and summary, instead of copying messages from the chat. It will be useful as any test of turn-based communication with the model, where the final result appears only after 5-6 rounds of questions and answers.

Second mode: contextual document versus real code

The second mode is an approach to projects with a certain base - for example, those built in 10xDevs, where you have prd, a database of changes in the archive status, or those implemented on the roadmap.

The agent can search through all business-oriented contextual documents, extract the canonical domain language from them, and then check whether you use concepts in the code that were mentioned at the initial stage of requirements gathering.

Use this prompt for language distillation:

You work as a Domain-Driven Design specialist focused on distilling the business domain from existing source documents. Your product is a MAP of the domain, not code. Do not assume any names of entities, aggregates, paths or requirement numbers in advance — you are supposed to DISCOVER them. Work in three steps: discovery → analysis → classification.

STEP 0 — Discover the project context.
- Find and read the vision/requirements documents, if they exist: look for prd.md,
  tech-stack.md, README (typically in the foundation/docs documents directory or in the
  repo root). If an extended narrative/change history exists — read it
  too as source material.
- If there are no requirements documents — rely on the README + code and clearly
  note this as a limitation.
- Determine the stack and repo structure: where the business logic lives (layers: API/
  service/domain/UI/persistence), what the source directories are.

STEP 1 — Build the Ubiquitous Language.
- Extract domain concepts from the documents AND from the code (names of entities,
  objects, states, operations, rules). Do NOT invent — cite the source.
- For each concept provide: a definition, a source quote (file:line), and where
  the term lives in the code (file:line) OR an explicit annotation "ABSENT from the code".

STEP 2 — Classify the subdomains: Core / Supporting / Generic.
- A table: assign each concept/area to one category and justify it
  by referring to the product goals (success criteria / vision section / non-goals,
  if they exist). The core = what constitutes the product's advantage and purpose.

STEP 3 — Identify aggregate candidates and their invariants.
- For each candidate: what business rule MUST always be true
  (the invariant), with a quote from the source, and the status: whether the code enforces it,
  declares it, or ignores it.

STEP 4 — Build a list of MODEL vs CODE divergences.
- A table: the document says X — the code does Y — evidence (file:line). This is the most valuable
  part: it shows where domain knowledge exists but the code does not reflect it.

STEP 5 — Refactoring ranking.
- Rank the aggregate candidates by value (how core the invariant is)
  and risk (how weakly it is enforced today). Point out #1 for refactoring and why.

CONSTRAINTS:
- Do not write production code. Cite only paths/lines you have actually
  verified.
- Save the document to: context/domain/01-domain-distillation.md
  (with frontmatter: title, created, type: domain-distillation).
- At the end, return a 5–8 sentence summary: what the artifact contains and the key takeaway.

Save the result to context/domain/01-domain-distillation.md

The result is a reference domain dictionary along with potential areas for refactoring.

Why is it worth doing this on evolving code rather than on an empty repository? A pragmatic view of DDD assumes that at the start of the project you still have too little knowledge about what you are actually doing. You get to know the domain, which every day turns out to be deeper and more engaging. The same goes for language – when the cost of initial mistakes is relatively low, extensive interviews and glossaries are not necessary. It’s a different matter in larger projects, when suddenly Account starts flying everywhere and no one knows what we are actually fixing – a user profile, an account, or accounting records.

Later in the lesson, you will see the results of such a prompt for 10xCards.

Invariants, or rules you must enforce

Domain distillation gives you a map. It shows where the language of the document diverges from the language of the code and which entities are candidates for something more than a loose row in the database. But the map is just a diagnosis. Now we go one level deeper – to a specific rule that in your domain must always be true, regardless of who and how interacts with the system.

We call such a rule an invariant. "The BankAccount balance never goes below the allowed overdraft." "An order cannot be shipped until it is paid." "A flashcard goes to the review only after its due date has passed." These are not form validations – these are the rules on which the product's meaning stands. If any of these break, the data will still be saved, but the business logic ceases to be valid.

The problem with invariants in legacy systems is always the same: the rule is everywhere and nowhere. Part of the validation lives in the UI component, another part in the service, the third is "guaranteed" by the order of calls in some handler, and nothing in the database enforces it. Most often, the only real guardian becomes the client – the part of the system that is easiest to bypass. And when the rule breaks, the error is silently swallowed instead of stopping the operation.

Let's go back to flashcard generation in 10xCards. The PRD describes a "generation session" as an entity with a lifecycle and a finalization moment. The natural invariant of this session sounds like: a session is only finalized when every generated proposal has been resolved – either accepted or rejected. However, as we established earlier, this entity does not exist in the code. Since there is no GenerationSession, there is also no place that enforces this rule (services or a few lines in some random function are incidental, not a strict principle). The UI counts accepted cards, the endpoint trusts whatever comes in the request, and the database holds a loose identifier with the proposal rows. The rule exists in the team's minds and in the document, but not in the code.

DDD has its tactical answer to this: aggregate. An aggregate is a consistency boundary - a single entity that is the only guardian of its invariant. Everything that wants to change its state must go through its methods, and a method that tries to break the rule throws a named domain error instead of silently saving an inconsistency.

IMAGE — https://images.przeprogramowani.pl/images/2026/06/Q4YlaqOjLLvOnsEPz_3P6A0.jpeg

Extracting such an invariant from legacy into a guard-aggregate is a task you can also delegate to an agent. This time you don't ask for a map, but for a refactor plan that will point out the most important rule and design its enforcement in one place:

You work as a Domain-Driven Design specialist focused on identifying and protecting domain invariants. The product is a refactoring PLAN, not an implementation — do not modify production code. Do not assume in advance which invariant is core or what the entities are called — you are supposed to DISCOVER and CHOOSE them. Work in steps: discovery → identification → classification → diagnosis → design.

STEP 0 — Discover the context.
- Read the requirements documents, if they exist (prd.md / tech-stack.md / README;
  look in foundation/docs/root). Pay attention to the "business logic" and
  "success criteria" sections, rules and functional requirements.
- Determine the stack and the layers where the business logic lives (API / service / domain /
  UI / persistence).

STEP 1 — IDENTIFY the business invariants.
- Build a list of rules that MUST always be true in this domain
  (e.g. "X is created only from Y", "operation Z is atomic", "data D is never
  persisted", "the A→B state transition requires condition C"). Extract them from the documents
  AND from the code. Cite the source.

STEP 2 — CLASSIFY and choose #1.
- For each invariant assess three axes:
  (a) how core it is to the product's purpose (refer to the goals/vision),
  (b) how much it is smeared across the layers (how many files/layers it lives in),
  (c) whether it is actually ENFORCED, merely declared, or violable.
- Choose the invariant that is at once the most core AND the most weakly
  enforced. Justify the choice.

STEP 3 — DIAGNOSE the chosen invariant.
- Show exactly where the rule lives today (file:line quotes across all
  layers). Point out: which layers do not enforce it, where it is enforced
  inconsistently, where the client (UI) is the only guardian, where an error is
  "swallowed" instead of stopping the operation.

STEP 4 — DESIGN the guardian aggregate.
- Design an aggregate (root) that is the ONLY place where the invariant is enforced.
- Domain methods with preconditions; an illegal operation throws a named domain
  error (it does not silently update state). Show signatures + pseudocode.
- A repository that loads/saves the aggregate instead of scattered queries; if
  the invariant requires atomicity — show how the whole thing runs in ONE transaction.
- A thin API/route: parse the input → aggregate method → map the domain error
  to a response. Enforcement moves from the client to the server (if today it sits on
  the client).

STEP 5 — Before/after, plan, tests.
- Before/after for every place the rule lives today.
- A plan of refactoring phases. If the project has a test-first discipline / an existing runner —
  mark which phases go test-first and list the test cases for the invariant
  (legal and illegal transitions/operations).
- A list of new "load-bearing" names to register, if the project keeps
  a contract registry.

CONSTRAINTS:
- Fail-fast: an illegal operation stops, it does not log-and-carry-on.
- Cite only verified file:line.
- Save the document to: context/domain/02-invariant-aggregate-refactor.md
  (frontmatter: title, created, type: refactor-plan).
- Return a 5–8 sentence summary at the end.

Save the result to context/domain/02-invariant-aggregate-refactor.md

Note that the prompt does not assume anything upfront. We do not provide the agent with the name of the entity nor do we say "fix the generation session." It is the agent who has to discover the list of invariants from documents and code, evaluate each on three axes – how core it is, how widespread it is, and how poorly enforced it is – and then choose the one that is both the most important and the worst enforced. This is intentional. Often the rule that hurts the most is not the one you suspect at first glance.

Next, the agent stops being an analyst and becomes a designer. It diagnoses where the rule currently resides (with quotes file:line), indicates layers that do not enforce it, and places where the error is swallowed. Then it designs an aggregate: methods with preconditions, named domain errors, a repository that loads and saves the whole, and if the invariant requires atomicity - everything within one transaction. Finally, you receive before/after for each place where the rule was scattered, a refactor phase plan, and a list of test cases for legal and illegal operations.

What does this give us? The rule stops being a convention that everyone on the team "remembers" and becomes code that cannot be bypassed. Enforcement moves from the client to the server, errors stop the operation instead of silently breaking it, and all the knowledge about when a session can be finalized ends up in one file, not four. For a project beyond MVP that you're scaling under pressure, it's the difference between a database you trust and one you rescue with a migration script every week.

Anti-Corruption Layer, or Against Pests

The invariant guarded the rule within your domain. Now let's look at the boundary with what is outside - external libraries, SDKs, and APIs on which your project depends but which you do not control.

Each such dependency has its own language and its own data shape. And as long as it stays in one place, everything is fine. The problem starts when it begins to leak - when its types and concepts spread through your code and mix with your domain.

Let's return once again to 10xCards and reviews. Earlier, we talked with an expert about SRS algorithms - Leitner, SM-2, FSRS. Let's assume the project reached for a ready-made FSRS library, for example ts-fsrs. This library has its own concepts: Card with its fields stability, difficulty, due, state, as well as its own rating enums. Convenient to start with. But after a few weeks, it turns out that the Card type from ts-fsrs sits in the database schema, returns in the API response, lands in the props of a Svelte component, and the logic of converting the "raw library object → something the UI can display" is duplicated in three places. Your domain flashcard and the library's Card are now one and the same object.

This is exactly a leak. And it has two costs. The first: when you want to replace ts-fsrs with another scheduler or your own SM-2, you will touch the entire system - database, API, and UI - because everywhere the shape of a specific library lives. The second, more dangerous: a library designed for calculating the schedule can end up in the client bundle, even though it has no business being there.

DDD calls the remedy Anti-Corruption Layer (ACL). It is a thin layer at the boundary with a dependency that translates its language into yours and back—so that the rest of the code never touches the foreign shape directly. In practice, this boils down to two things: a domain value object, which is the only place with knowledge of the dependency's shape, and a narrow port—an interface described in your domain language—that is implemented by an adapter wrapping the specific library. The rest of the code knows only the port.

IMAGE — https://images.przeprogramowani.pl/images/2026/06/V4YlasuTDszPnsEPxo7ViQ4.jpeg

You can delegate the identification of the worst leak and the ACL design to the agent again:

You work as a Domain-Driven Design specialist focused on identifying leaking dependencies and broken domain layer boundaries. The product is a refactoring PLAN, not an implementation — do not modify production code. Do not assume in advance which dependency leaks or what the entities are called — you are supposed to DISCOVER and CHOOSE them. Steps: discovery → identification → classification → diagnosis → design.

STEP 0 — Discover the context.
- Read the base documents, if they exist (prd.md / tech-stack.md / README).
  Pay attention to declarations about the replaceability of components or about some
  entity being deliberately separated "so that X can be swapped out".
- Determine the stack, the list of external dependencies (the package manifest) and the code layers.

STEP 1 — IDENTIFY the leaking dependencies.
- Find external dependencies that leak across layer boundaries. Signals:
  the same package imported in multiple layers (API + UI + service), duplicated
  reconstruction of the library's objects/types in several places, library types in
  domain signatures or in wire contracts (DTO/response), calling the same
  SDK on both sides of the client/server boundary.
- For each one: list ALL the files that "know" it today (file:line).

STEP 2 — CLASSIFY and choose #1.
- Assess each axis: (a) the number of layers/files affected, (b) the risk/cost of replacing
  the library today, (c) whether the documents declare it should be replaceable (an
  intent-vs-code divergence is a strong signal). Choose the worst leak. Justify it.

STEP 3 — DIAGNOSIS.
- Show the duplication (file:line quotes) and the leaks across boundaries — especially
  the dangerous ones (e.g. a server library pulled into the client bundle). If a document
  declares replaceability — quote it (file:line) and show that the code does not honor it.

STEP 4 — DESIGN the ACL.
- Design a domain value object/entity that is the ONLY place that knows the
  dependency's shape (mapping from/to persistence, conversion to/from the library
  type, domain operations). Show signatures + pseudocode.
- Define a NARROW port (a domain interface) and an adapter implementing it with the
  concrete library. The rest of the code knows only the port.

STEP 5 — Proof of isolation + before/after.
- Prove with a list that swapping the library touches only the adapter, not the tables/API/UI.
- Before/after for the duplicated places; show that the UI layer receives ready
  domain data, not a raw library object.
- If there are open questions that depend on this library's contract — resolve
  them based on its documentation and indicate where to encode the decision (in the ACL, not in
  the API layer).

STEP 6 — Verification and plan.
- Success criterion: a grep for the package name returns only files in the ACL/
  adapter directory. List which files know the dependency today, and which no longer will after the refactor.
- A phase plan consistent with the project's conventions.

CONSTRAINTS:
- Cite only verified file:line. Do not write production code.
- Save the document to: context/domain/03-anti-corruption-layer.md
  (frontmatter: title, created, type: refactor-plan).
- Return a 5–8 sentence summary at the end.

Save the result to context/domain/03-anti-corruption-layer.md

The prompt mechanics are nearly identical to those for invariants, but it looks for something different. The agent first scans the package manifests and layers to discover leaking dependencies — the signal is the same package imported simultaneously in the API, UI, and service, library types in contracts sent over the network, or duplicated reconstruction of its objects. Then it classifies and selects the worst leak, considering among other things whether the documents declared interchangeability that the code did not maintain — because the discrepancy between intention and code is the strongest signal. Only then does it design the value object, port, and adapter, and shows before/after for each duplicated spot.

The most interesting part here is the way the prompt defines success. It doesn't ask "does it look nicer," but rather provides a verifiable criterion: after the refactor, grep by package name should return only files in the adapter directory. This is a criterion you can verify yourself in a second and that the agent must design the entire plan around. Replacing the library stops being a project for the whole sprint and becomes just swapping out one adapter — the rest of the domain won't even notice.

Interactive DDD Workshop with Event-Storming Canvas

Everything we have done so far - language distillation, invariants, ACL - is work that the agent performs on prepared material: on documents and code. But some domain knowledge has never been written down in any file. It resides in people's minds and only emerges when you put them together at a whiteboard and make them go through the process step by step. That is exactly what Event Storming is for.

Event Storming is a workshop technique created by Alberto Brandolini: the team models a business process by sticking colorful notes on the wall – orange ones are events (“Order placed”), blue ones are commands, yellow ones are actors, red ones are hotspots, meaning risk areas and open questions. The notes are arranged on a timeline and suddenly the entire process becomes visible, along with its gaps and places where no one knows “what actually happens when the payment fails”.

The problem is exactly the same as with the domain interview from the beginning of the lesson: the entry cost. Classic Event Storming requires booking a room, a wall full of sticky notes, a domain expert, several developers, and a moderator who oversees the method and prevents the group from gluing everything into one big note. It's a full day of work for several people - for a post-MVP refactoring that the client might not even notice, which is rarely justifiable.

And again: the expert's agent does not replace it. But it can be a first approach at a "good enough" level—one that alone, after hours, extracts 80% of the knowledge from the process before you invite real people to the table. An agent equipped with the right rules can take on the role of an Event Storming moderator: guiding you through the next phases, adding sample events, merging duplicates, asking tough questions, and turning your "I'm not sure who approves this" into a red hotspot on the board.

To make this workshop possible to conduct visually, and not only in chat, we have prepared a small tool: event-storming-canvas.

  • Event Storming Canvas - https://github.com/przeprogramowani/event-storming-canvas

What is event-storming-canvas

This is a simple application (pure Node.js, no npm install, no build step), which is a practical example of a solution where a human and an agent work on the same state in real time.

This shared state is a single file - board.json - which is the single source of truth for the entire board. It works like this:

  • You run the workshop in the browser: you add, move, and edit sticky notes, switch phases.
  • Agent reads and edits board.json - "thinks on the board": adds events, arranges them on the timeline, marks hotspots, asks questions.
  • The table refreshes live in every connected browser thanks to Server-Sent Events. Changes in the file are visible immediately, without reloading.

Importantly, the tool is not just a regular drawing board. It has specific frameworks: it knows Brandolini's grammar (colors matter, each role has its lane), and the phase selector - from chaotic-exploration, through timeline and hotspots, up to aggregates - restricts the toolbar to roles appropriate for the given stage. The method itself forces you to follow the sequence: first the event storm, then commands, actors, and aggregates. It's hard to take shortcuts here.

How to use this

Getting started is simple - you only need Node.js:

node server.js
# open http://localhost:4000

The second step is setting the agent in the role of moderator. The repository has a CLAUDE.md (and its counterpart AGENTS.md), which contains the full moderator protocol: how to clear the board at the start, how to respect the current phase, how not to overwrite your sticky notes, and how to turn ambiguities into hotspots. These rules transform the ordinary assistant into a workshop facilitator - exactly the same "project rules" mechanism that you know from previous modules.

Next, the workshop is just a short dialogue. You say what you want; the agent edits board.json and reports in the chat what it did. For the flashcard generation process from 10xCards, it could look like this:

Clear the board and run an Event Storming workshop for the process
of asynchronous flashcard generation in 10xCards. Start with the
chaotic-exploration phase and add 4–5 domain events in the past tense.

Move to the hotspots phase and mark in red the places where
the process can blow up — a model error, a partially accepted session,
a generation timeout.

The agent always first reads board.json, so it builds on your current state and does not delete what you have already added. After a few rounds, you have the whole process in front of you: events on the timeline, commands and actors above them, and red hotspots in risky spots – a ready list of candidates for further analysis of invariants and context boundaries. Importantly, sessions have no memory: there is no database or history, each workshop starts with a clean slate, and its entire state is board.json. If you want to save the result, you simply save this file.

With each round, the code gets closer to how the business actually works - and that is exactly why it remains maintainable in a week, a month, and a quarter.

When to Reach for DDD (and Why Not From Day One)

Since DDD offers so much, the question arises: why not start with it on day one, even before the first line of code?

Because it depends – mainly on how well you know the domain from the start. If you are entering an area that you have down pat, or you have a dedicated domain expert on your side for the project, early modeling makes sense – you map the knowledge you actually possess.

It looks different in a solo project or in a new market, where you are just getting to know the domain, and the toughest teacher will be the reaction of the first customers. Here the domain is shallow, the language fresh, and most of the "hard" decisions about aggregates and boundaries are actually guesses. You are then modeling not reality, but your first impression of it.

And the vision from day one is almost always wrong. In such a setup, it is more important to get out into the world as quickly as possible and check if the client even expects it, rather than having a perfectly modeled domain from the first day. First market validation, then—once you know there is something to maintain—refactoring based on real knowledge.

There is also a second, purely economic argument. At an early stage, the cost of a wrong name is low - you have three files, the change takes a minute. An extensive domain interview and glossary at this stage is an investment in knowledge, half of which will become outdated before you have a chance to use it.

That is why the pragmatic approach to DDD reverses the classic order. Instead of "model first, then code," it goes more like: code, learn the domain, and model only when the domain starts to hurt.

And how do you know it’s starting to hurt? By a few quite specific signals:

  • The same term means several different things in the project - the classic 3xAccount, where nobody knows anymore whether AccountService touches the profile, account, or ledger.
  • The entity from PRD has no counterpart in the code - the rule "the session only ends when every proposal is resolved" exists in the team's minds, but is not visible anywhere.
  • Every week you rescue the database with a migration script because the data gets into a state that "should not happen".
  • Replacing one library turns out to be a project for the entire sprint because its types have spread throughout the whole system.

Each of these points is the same message: the domain is now deep enough that it's worth finally naming it. And this is precisely the moment from the main part of the lesson – the "post-MVP" stage, the second big cycle, where the architecture is shaped by the domain, not intuition. Not the first day of the project, but the first day when the project starts to resist.

Five Myths about DDD

DDD has the reputation of being a heavy, "corporate" methodology for architects after training sessions. This reputation is off-putting and largely stems from misunderstandings. Here are the five most common ones – and what the reality looks like.

Myth 1: DDD is a heavy architecture - layers, repositories, aggregates at five levels.tactical DDD, which is only half of it. The other, strategic - ubiquitous language, bounded contexts, subdomain division - does not require a single new class for communication and understanding of the project to reach a higher level.

Myth 2: DDD is only for large systems and microservices.3xAccount, not at the hundredth microservice.

Myth 3: Without a domain expert in the team, there is nothing to talk about.

Myth 4: DDD equals Event Storming (or: DDD equals aggregates).discovering the domain, an aggregate is one of its enforcements patterns. Neither of them is "the entire DDD." It's like confusing a hammer with building a house. In this lesson, we used both, but as tools for the same goal - to make the code speak the same language as the business.

Myth 5: either you deploy everything, or it makes no sense.

The common denominator of these five myths is one: they confuse discipline (make sure the code reflects the business) with ceremony (do it in this specific, cumbersome way). AI reduces the cost of ceremony almost to zero – and discipline has always been cheap.

When the domain expert makes things up

The "domain expert interview" mode is based on one assumption: that the model really knows the domain. Most often it does – but it also has its characteristic failure mode, namely hallucinations. And in this application, they are particularly insidious.

Why is it tricky? Because you’re asking about something you don’t really know well yourself by definition. When you ask the model for code in a framework you know, you’ll catch nonsense. When you ask “how FSRS really works,” you have no frame of reference, so a smooth and confident answer looks like knowledge—even if the model just made up half the details.

It is worth knowing where the model is strong and where it starts to confabulate.

It is strong where the domain is broadly described, and the general question: what are flashcards, how Leitner differs from SM-2, roughly what the journey of a package from sender to recipient looks like. These concepts are "memorized" from hundreds of sources.

It starts to fabricate details that sound precise: specific formulas and constants of the algorithm, exact thresholds and intervals, field names in someone else's API, recent changes in the tool, niche regulated domains (taxes, law, industry standards). The more concrete and "numerical" the answer, the more cautious you should be.

Note that even in this lesson, the language around algorithms is deliberately cautious - “Anki used for years a variant inspired by SM-2,” “FSRS implementations describe it as a model based on...”. This is not accidental. This is how a sentence that has passed cross-check looks: it leaves room for doubt where there is no certainty.

Because the remedy for expert hallucination is simple and cheap: treat the interview as a hypothesis generator, not as a source of truth. The glossary and processes that come out of the conversation are a great starting point – but verify every claim you plan to base your code on (load-bearing claim) before it goes into a diagram or class name.

You do cross-checking in three ways, from the cheapest:

  • Search engine and source documentation - for field names, patterns, and flags, go directly to the official library docs or specifications. This is the only place where the details are truly reliable.
  • Two models instead of one - ask the same question to two different models. Where they agree, the risk is lower; where they diverge, you have a ready-made list of things to manually check. This is, after all, one of the reasons why agent-forum places models from two different providers face to face.
  • Confrontation with your own code and PRD - the second mode from this lesson, "document versus real code," is itself a form of cross-check. The model does not then declare "this is how the world works," but quotes file:line from your repo, which you can open and check in a second.

An expert with LLM is like a very well-read consultant who never says "I don't know". Use their knowledge, but leave the final word to a source that can be accessed.
